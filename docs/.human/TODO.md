1. Inventory everything that arrived.
   Record the printer model, nozzle, build plate, filament types, exact speaker model numbers, fasteners, inserts, gasket materials, terminals, wiring, adhesives, and measurement equipment. Inspect the drivers for shipping damage.

2. Commission the printer.
   Follow its manufacturer setup/calibration procedure, then produce one small calibration print. Record the slicer profile, filament, dimensions, adhesion, and visible defects.

3. Verify the physical design inputs.
   Measure the woofer, midrange, and tweeter frames, cutouts, mounting holes, depths, terminals, and gasket faces. Compare those measurements with the CAD and datasheets.

4. Recheck the vehicle envelope.
   Confirm seat travel, HVAC clearance, passenger-foot clearance, cable routing, and potential structural restraint points. The existing 415 mm model length has only 5 mm nominal printer-bed margin.

5. Confirm the prototype material.
   PLA is acceptable for coupons and temporary fit checks, but not the final road-use enclosure. The road-use material needs adequate parked-car temperature resistance.

6. Resume focused CAD work.
   Implement one removable, gasketed side panel. Don’t combine this revision with tweeter, crossover, and binding-post integration.

7. Print feature coupons.
   Test driver cutouts, threaded inserts, gasket compression, panel joints, binding posts, PG7 glands, and the Sorbothane pocket before printing the cabinet.

8. Freeze and review V1.
   Incorporate coupon results, calculate net chamber volumes, model the woofer alignment, confirm slicer fit, and review print time, material consumption, supports, orientation, and late-failure risk.

9. Print and bench-assemble.
   Leak-test and inspect the enclosure before powering drivers or installing it in the vehicle. Occupied-road use must wait for a positive mechanical restraint.
